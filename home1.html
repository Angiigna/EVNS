Flora of Pondicherry University: A Green Haven

Pondicherry University boasts a rich and diverse range of plant species, contributing to its green and serene environment. These plants not only enhance the beauty of the campus but also serve ecological and medicinal purposes. Below are some notable trees and plants found within the university grounds:

Red Frangipani (Plumeria rubra)

Origin: Mexico, Central America

Location: Opposite to Boy's Tea Time

Known for its vibrant red flowers, this tropical beauty is often used in traditional rituals and as an ornamental plant.

Bamboo

Origin: Asia, South America, Africa

Coordinates: 12.030723, 79.858859

Bamboo is a fast-growing species that plays a crucial role in preventing soil erosion and providing habitat for various wildlife.

Sausage Tree (Kigelia africana)

Origin: Tropical Africa

Coordinates: 12.030723, 79.858859

Named after its unique sausage-shaped fruits, this tree is valued for its medicinal properties, particularly for skin treatments.

Touch-Me-Not (Mimosa pudica)

Origin: South and Central America

Coordinates: 12.030723, 79.858859

A fascinating plant that reacts to touch by folding its leaves. It is widely used in Ayurveda for its anti-inflammatory properties.

Copper Pod (Peltophorum pterocarpum)

Origin: South and Southeast Asia

Coordinates: 12.030633, 79.858880

This tree is recognized for its golden-yellow flowers and is often planted along roadsides as an ornamental shade tree.

African Tulip Tree (Spathodea campanulata)

Origin: West Africa

Coordinates: 12.031510, 79.860296

An exotic tree with striking red-orange flowers that attract birds and pollinators.

Empress Tree (Paulownia tomentosa)

Origin: China

Coordinates: 12.033173, 79.859959

A fast-growing tree known for its large, heart-shaped leaves and violet flowers, widely cultivated for timber.

Neem Tree (Azadirachta indica)

Origin: Indian Subcontinent

Coordinates: 12.034057, 79.857298

A sacred tree known for its antibacterial and antifungal properties, used in traditional medicine and organic farming.

Mango Tree (Mangifera indica)

Origin: South Asia

Coordinates: 12.016100, 79.853523

One of India's most beloved fruit trees, known for its delicious mangoes and lush green foliage.

Kapok Tree (Ceiba pentandra)

Origin: Central and South America

Coordinates: 12.017323, 79.853045

A towering tree known for its silky fibers used in stuffing pillows and mattresses.

Jamun Tree or Indian Blackberry

Origin: Indian Subcontinent, Southeast Asia

Coordinates:12.020798, 79.855852

The Jamun Tree is an evergreen tree with a dense canopy of elliptical leaves and clusters of small, round fruits. Its deep purple hue and unique sweet-tart taste make Jamun a sought-after treat in various culinary delights.

Yellow Silk Cotton Tree or Cochlospermum gossypium

Origin: Tropical regions of Indian Subcontinent, Southeast Asia

Coordinates:12.021068, 79.855660

A flowering plant growing to a height of 7.5 m (25 ft) usually found in dry deciduous forests Another common name is buttercup tree.In Theravada Buddhism, this plant is said to have used as the tree for achieved enlightenment.

Pink Trumpet Tree or Tabebuia rosea tree

Origin: Native to Central and South America

Coordinates:12.021618,79.855919

Tabebuia rosea, also called pink poui, and rosy trumpet tree is a neotropical tree that grows up to 30 m (98 ft) and can reach a diameter at breast height of up to 100 cm (3 ft).

Dypsis lutescens or Areca Palm

Origin: Native to Madagascar

Coordinates:12.021618,79.855919

Dypsis lutescens most commonly known as areca palm is a beautiful palm plant and is a very popular ornamental all over the world. It is a very hardy plant and can survive in almost all weather conditions. It is particularly popular for its unique nature of giving out oxygen during the night time and also helps in purifying the air inside.

Badminton Ball Tree (Parkia biglandulosa)

Origin: Southeast Asia

Coordinates:12.033173, 79.859959

Commonly found near academic buildings, this tree produces spherical flower heads that resemble badminton shuttlecocks, giving it its common name. The tree can grow up to 30 meters tall and has compound leaves that fold at night. Its pods contain edible seeds that are used in certain traditional dishes in Southeast Asia.

Indian Cork Tree (Millingtonia hortensis)

Origin: Myanmar and Thailand

Coordinates:12.03406, 79.856588

Also known as the Tree Jasmine, this tall evergreen tree produces fragrant white flowers, especially during the night. It can grow up to 25 meters tall with a straight trunk. The bark is used in traditional medicine to treat asthma and other respiratory conditions. The light, cork-like bark gives it its common name.

Indian Butter Tree (Madhuca longifolia)

Origin: Indian Subcontinent

Coordinates:12.01731, 79.85265

Also known as Mahua, this tree produces edible flowers that are fermented to make alcoholic beverages in tribal areas. The seeds yield butter-like oil used in cooking, soap-making, and traditional medicine. It can grow up to 20 meters tall and is considered sacred in many Indian communities.

Cherry Tree (Prunus species)

Origin: Depends on species, many from Asia and Europe

Coordinates:12.016149, 79.85415

This beautiful flowering tree produces an abundance of blossoms, creating a stunning visual display during the flowering season. The specific variety at this location adds ornamental value to the campus with its seasonal blooms and compact growth habit.

Madre de Cacao (Gliricidia sepium)

Origin:Central America

Coordinates:12.03406, 79.856588

A multipurpose tree used in agroforestry systems, it produces pink to purple flowers and has nitrogen-fixing properties that improve soil fertility. Growing to 10-12 meters tall, it's commonly used as living fences and shade trees for coffee and cacao plantations. All parts of the plant are toxic to rodents, giving it another common name, "Rat Killer."

Banyan Tree (Ficus benghalensis)

Origin:Indian Subcontinent

Coordinates:12.01636, 79.85378

India's national tree, known for its aerial prop roots that grow into thick woody trunks. It can spread over several acres, creating a mini-ecosystem supporting numerous life forms. Some banyan trees are hundreds of years old and have cultural and spiritual significance in Hinduism and Buddhism.

Wild Indian Almond (Sterculia foetida)

Origin:East Africa, South and Southeast Asia

Coordinates:12.015963, 79.854473

This large deciduous tree produces star-shaped pods containing edible seeds. The tree can grow up to 40 meters tall with a straight trunk and horizontally spreading branches. Despite its name "foetida" (meaning foul-smelling), it's valued for its timber and medicinal properties.

Pilkhan Tree (Ficus virens)

Origin:South and Southeast Asia

Coordinates:12.0166, 79.85318

Also known as the White Fig, this tree starts life as an epiphyte, eventually sending roots to the ground and sometimes strangling its host. It produces small fig fruits that are popular with birds and wildlife. The tree can grow to 30 meters tall and has medicinal uses in traditional Ayurvedic medicine.

Teak (Tectona grandis)

Origin:South and Southeast Asia

Coordinates: 12.015774, 79.854724

Prized for its highly durable wood that's resistant to termites, fungal decay, and water damage. The tree can grow up to 40 meters tall with large, ovate leaves. Teak wood is one of the most valuable timbers worldwide, used in high-quality furniture, shipbuilding, and decorative objects.

Flame Tree (Delonix regia)

Origin:Madagascar

Coordinates: 12.016723, 79.85354

Also known as Royal Poinciana or Flamboyant, this tree produces spectacular displays of fiery red-orange blooms. It grows up to 12 meters tall with a wide-spreading umbrella-shaped canopy and delicate fern-like leaves. It's known as one of the most colorful trees in the world during its flowering period.

Earpod Tree (Enterolobium cyclocarpum)

Origin:Central and South America

Coordinates:12.021086, 79.854311

Named after its distinctive ear-shaped seed pods, this massive tree can grow up to 35 meters tall with a broad, umbrella-shaped crown. It's one of the largest trees in the Americas, with a trunk diameter reaching up to 3 meters. The wood is used in furniture making, and the tree has nitrogen-fixing properties beneficial for soil.

Autograph Tree (Clusia rosea)

Origin:Caribbean

Coordinates:12.021604, 79.853932

Also known as the Pitch Apple, this evergreen tree gets its common name from the ease with which one can scratch messages onto its thick, leathery leaves. It produces large white flowers with pink centers and starts life as an epiphyte, eventually sending down aerial roots. It's considered invasive in some Pacific islands.

Tamarind Tree (Tamarindus indica)

Origin: Africa

Coordinates:12.02526, 79.85082

Despite its name suggesting Indian origin, the tamarind originated in Africa but has been cultivated in India for thousands of years. The tree produces pod-like fruits with a sweet-sour pulp used extensively in Asian and Latin American cuisines. It can grow up to 30 meters tall and live for over 200 years.

Peepal Tree (Ficus religiosa)

Origin:Indian Subcontinent

Coordinates:12.02192, 79.85295

Also known as the Sacred Fig or Bodhi Tree, it has deep religious significance in Hinduism, Buddhism, and Jainism. Buddha is said to have attained enlightenment under this tree. It has distinctive heart-shaped leaves with elongated tips and can live for hundreds of years, reaching heights of up to 30 meters.

White Trumpet Vine (Brugmansia suaveolens)

Origin:South America

Coordinates:12.021372, 79.853283

Also called Angel's Trumpet, this woody shrub or small tree produces large, fragrant, hanging trumpet-shaped flowers that bloom at night. All parts of the plant are highly toxic, containing tropane alkaloids. It can grow up to 5 meters tall and was traditionally used by shamans in South America for its hallucinogenic properties.

Bougainvillea (Bougainvillea species)

Origin:South America

Coordinates:12.02289, 79.85247

Often called paperflower, this popular ornamental vine is known for its vibrant paper-like modified leaves (bracts) in shades of pink, purple, red, orange, or white. The true flowers are small and usually white. Bougainvillea is drought-tolerant, can be trained as a climbing vine or pruned into a shrub, and blooms nearly year-round in tropical climates.

Lipstick Tree (Bixa orellana)

Origin: Tropical Americas

Coordinates:12.02308, 79.84727

Named for the bright red-orange dye extracted from its seeds, which has been used as body paint, food coloring, and lipstick. The small tree or shrub produces pretty pink flowers and spiny heart-shaped seed pods. The natural dye, annatto, is still widely used as a culinary colorant in foods like cheese, butter, and rice dishes.

Dragon Bones/Candelabra Plant (Euphorbia lactea)

Origin:Southeast Asia and India

Coordinates:12.022316, 79.847822

This succulent cactus-like plant has a distinctive candelabra-shaped growth pattern with upright, branching stems featuring three ribs with small spines. Often cultivated with crested forms that resemble brain coral. Like other euphorbias, it contains a toxic white latex. It can grow up to 5 meters tall in ideal conditions.

Chiku/Sapodilla (Manilkara zapota)

Origin:Central America and southern Mexico

Coordinates:12.0214, 79.8496

An evergreen tree that produces sweet, brown fruits with a flavor similar to pears and brown sugar. The tree can grow up to 30 meters tall and once was the source of chicle, the original base for chewing gum. It has dark, glossy leaves and can live for over 100 years while continuing to produce fruit.

Mimosa Tree (Albizia julibrissin)

Origin:Southwest and Eastern Asia

Coordinates:12.026192, 79.851797

Also known as the Silk Tree or Persian Silk Tree, this small deciduous tree produces delicate pink, silky thread-like flowers that resemble pom-poms. The leaves are fern-like and fold at night or when touched, similar to the Touch-Me-Not plant. It grows quickly to about 12 meters tall and has a broad, spreading crown.

River Tamarind (Leucaena leucocephala)

Origin:Southern Mexico and Northern Central America

Coordinates:12.01882, 79.850851

A fast-growing small tree that produces white, globular flowers and flat seed pods. It's extensively used in agroforestry as it fixes nitrogen in the soil. While valuable for erosion control and as a protein-rich fodder crop, it's considered invasive in many tropical regions due to its aggressive spreading nature.

Foxtail Palm (Wodyetia bifurcata)

Origin: Australia

Coordinates:12.02692, 79.852961

Named for its distinctive fronds that resemble a fox's tail, this elegant palm was only discovered by science in the 1970s. It can grow up to 10 meters tall with a smooth, gray trunk. The palm produces bright red fruits and is widely cultivated as an ornamental in tropical and subtropical regions worldwide.

Azalea Plant (Rhododendron species)

Origin:Varies by species, many from Asia, North America, and Europe

Coordinates:12.02789, 79.85435

These flowering shrubs are known for their spectacular spring blooms in shades of pink, red, purple, white, and orange. Technically all azaleas are rhododendrons, though they tend to be smaller with thinner leaves. They prefer acidic soil and partial shade and are widely cultivated for their ornamental value.

Rain Tree (Samanea saman)

Origin:Central and South America

Coordinates:12.034022, 79.856566

Also known as the Monkey Pod Tree, this massive shade tree can grow up to 25 meters tall with a canopy spread of 30 meters or more. It gets its name because the leaves fold at night and during rain. The tree produces pink puffball flowers and woody pods containing edible seeds covered in sweet pulp.

Red Sandalwood

Origin: Southern Asia and Pacific Islands

Coordinates:12.034882, 79.857618

Also called Red Sandalwood or Red Bead Tree, this legume produces bright red seeds that are used as beads in jewelry and for traditional weight measurements (they each weigh almost exactly one grain/0.065g). The tree can grow up to 20 meters tall and provides valuable hardwood timber with a red heartwood.

Copper Leaf (Acalypha wilkesiana)

Origin: Pacific Islands

Coordinates:12.034353, 79.858749

A colorful ornamental shrub with large, heart-shaped leaves that display stunning patterns of red, copper, pink, and green. It can grow up to 3 meters tall and is commonly used as a hedge or accent plant in tropical landscapes. The plant produces small, inconspicuous flowers on catkin-like spikes.

Yellow Flame (Peltophorum pterocarpum)

Origin:Southeast Asia and Northern Australia

Coordinates:12.01844, 79.85074

Also known as Copper Pod or Yellow Poinciana, this medium to large deciduous tree produces masses of bright yellow flowers, creating a spectacular display when in bloom. It can grow up to 24 meters tall and is widely planted as a shade and ornamental tree throughout tropical regions. The wood is used for cabinetry and construction.


BINOMIAL NAME: Canis familiaris

The dog is a domesticated descendant of the gray wolf. Also called the domestic dog, it was selectively bred from an extinct population of wolves during the Late Pleistocene by hunter-gatherers. The dog was the first species to be domesticated by humans, over 14,000 years ago and before the development of agriculture. Due to their long association with humans, dogs have gained the ability to thrive on a starch-rich diet that would be inadequate for other canids.

ECOLOGICAL ROLE:

Natural Pest Control

Stray dogs help in rodent populations by preying on them, which keeps their population in check. It also reduces the need to spray chemical pesticides to kill these rodents, thereby promoting a healthier environment. Stray dogs also help in managing the population of small insects and other pests; this limits the spread of diseases and protects the crops from being damaged. Stray dogs role in balancing this process highlights their importance and the need to ensure that their population is effectively managed as we also acknowledge their ecological contributions.

Ecological Balancers

By preying on small insects and rodents, stray dogs play an important role in indirectly supporting the survival of various plants and animal species that otherwise can be impacted by these pests. Additionally, stray dogs scavenging role helps in the process of decomposition of organic waste. They consume these wastes, including animal carcasses, and aid in decomposition and nutrients cycling in the environment.

Human Benefits

In addition to their ecological role of reducing rodents and other pests, which help in reducing the spread of several diseases such as leptospirosis and hantavirus, stray dogs also offer social and emotional benefits to the communities. They can be informal companions to people, especially the lonely or those who live in isolation. Their presence also brings people together who shower them with care and affection. This enhances empathy and kindness in the community.

Common Dog Behavior Observations

Territorial Instincts: Dogs tend to establish territories around hostels and cafeterias, where they feel safe and can access food easily.

Routine Activities: Most dogs follow a predictable routine—roaming in groups, resting in shaded areas, and scavenging for food at specific times.

Social Groups: Dogs often form packs based on familiarity and hierarchy. Conflicts between groups usually occur over food or territory.

Friendly vs. Cautious Behavior: While some dogs are friendly towards students, others remain cautious, especially at night or when unfamiliar people approach.

Mother Dogs & Puppies: Female dogs with puppies are highly protective and prefer to stay in quiet, less-disturbed areas.

BINOMIAL NAME: Corvus splendens

The Crow is a highly intelligent bird belonging to the Corvidae family. Crows are known for their problem-solving abilities, tool use, and complex social behaviors. They are found all over the world and have adapted exceptionally well to urban and rural environments. Crows have long been associated with humans, often thriving in human settlements by scavenging and using our waste as a food source. Their remarkable memory and learning capacity make them one of the most fascinating bird species in the animal kingdom.

Scientific classification

  • Domain: Eukaryota
  • Kingdom: Animalia
  • Phylum: Chordata
  • Class: Aves
  • Order: Passeriformes
  • Family: Corvidae
  • Genus: Corvus
  • Species: Corvus splendens

ECOLOGICAL ROLE:

Natural Waste Managers
Crows are efficient scavengers. They feed on organic waste, carrion, and discarded food, reducing the volume of waste and helping to maintain cleanliness in urban environments. This natural waste management limits the spread of diseases and reduces the need for human-led waste cleanup.
Pest Control
Crows also help control the population of various insects, small rodents, and even agricultural pests by feeding on them. Their diet includes caterpillars, beetles, grasshoppers, and other crop-damaging organisms, which makes them beneficial in protecting agricultural ecosystems.
Seed Dispersal Agents
By consuming fruits and later excreting the seeds, crows contribute to plant propagation and biodiversity. Their movement across different habitats allows for wide dispersal of seeds, especially in fragmented environments.
Human Benefits
Crows play a unique role in human society by acting as environmental indicators. Their presence and behavior can signal changes in the ecosystem, such as pollution or food scarcity. In addition, their intelligence has fascinated researchers, educators, and nature lovers alike. Crows are known to recognize human faces, hold grudges, and even exchange “gifts” with people they trust. Socially, in some cultures, crows are associated with mythology, ancestral spirits, and omens. Observing and engaging with crows can foster curiosity, respect for wildlife, and awareness of ecological interdependence.

Common Crow Behavior Observations

Territorial Instincts: Crows often establish territories around tree clusters, rooftops, and food sources like garbage bins, canteens, and marketplaces. They defend these areas from other animals and rival crow groups.

Routine Activities:Crows typically follow a daily routine, scavenging for food in open areas, markets, and around human settlements, resting in shaded trees or high structures, often grooming themselves or socializing, gathering in large numbers (called a roost) at specific spots like tall trees or quiet areas for resting.

Social Groups:Crows live in social groups or flocks. These groups have a certain pecking order (hierarchy). Stronger, older crows dominates. Conflicts happen over food, space, or nesting spots. Crows often cooperate to chase away threats or alert each other to danger.

Nesting & Parenting: Crows build nests high up in trees or buildings using twigs, wires, and cloth pieces. Mother crows are highly protective of their nests and chicks. They often stay in quiet, undisturbed spots and can become aggressive if approached
